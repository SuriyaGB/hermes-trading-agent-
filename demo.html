SECTION 01 [cite: 27]

Navier-Stokes Equations [cite: 28]

Governing Equations of Fluid Motion [cite: 29]

"The mathematical foundation of all fluid dynamics" [cite: 30]

What Are the Navier-Stokes Equations? [cite: 31]

History · Significance · Applications [cite: 32]

  • Developed by Claude-Louis Navier (1822) and George Gabriel Stokes (1845) [cite: 33]
  • A system of Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) describing motion of viscous fluids [cite: 34]
  • Apply Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$) to every infinitesimal fluid parcel [cite: 35]
  • Fundamental to CFD, weather modeling, aerospace, ocean engineering & biomedical flows [cite: 36]
  • One of the 7 Millennium Prize Problems — no closed-form general solution proven yet [cite: 37]
  • Solved numerically via methods: FVM (Finite Volume), FEM, FDM in modern CFD codes [cite: 38]

Continuity Equation [cite: 39]

Conservation of Mass [cite: 40]

  • Principle: Mass cannot be created or destroyed within a fluid system [cite: 41]
  • Incompressible flow (constant density): $\nabla \cdot V = 0$ [cite: 42]
  • Compressible flow (variable density): $\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho V) = 0$ [cite: 43]
  • Physical meaning: what flows IN must flow OUT — mass is conserved at every point [cite: 44]
  • In pipe flow: $A_1V_1 = A_2V_2$ (smaller pipe → faster flow) [cite: 45]
Compressible: $\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho V) = 0$

Incompressible: $\nabla \cdot V = 0$ [cite: 46]
$\rho$ = density | $V$ = velocity vector | $t$ = time [cite: 47]

Momentum Equation [cite: 48]

Newton's 2nd Law for Fluids [cite: 49]

  • Inertial forces (LHS) = Pressure gradient + Viscous forces + Body forces (RHS) [cite: 50]
  • Left side: rate of change of momentum of a fluid parcel [cite: 52]
  • $-\nabla P$ : Pressure gradient drives flow from high → low pressure [cite: 53]
  • $\mu \nabla^2 V$ : Viscous (friction) forces resist deformation of fluid [cite: 54]
  • $\rho g$ : Body forces (gravity, Coriolis in geophysical flows) [cite: 55]
  • Non-linear term $V \cdot \nabla V$ makes analytical solutions nearly impossible [cite: 56]
$$\rho \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + V \cdot \nabla V\right) = -\nabla P + \mu \nabla^2 V + \rho g$$ [cite: 51, 57]
$\mu$ = dynamic viscosity | $P$ = pressure | $g$ = gravitational acceleration [cite: 58]

Energy Equation [cite: 59]

1st Law of Thermodynamics for Flowing Fluids [cite: 60]

  • Describes conservation of energy in a moving, heat-conducting fluid [cite: 61]
  • $DT/Dt$ : Material derivative — total rate of temperature change following fluid [cite: 63]
  • $k \nabla^2 T$ : Thermal diffusion (Fourier conduction within the fluid) [cite: 64]
  • $\Phi$ (Viscous dissipation): Kinetic energy converted to heat by viscosity [cite: 65]
  • $Q_{source}$: Internal heat generation (e.g. chemical reaction) [cite: 66]
  • Links velocity field (momentum) with temperature field [cite: 67]
$$\rho c_p \frac{DT}{Dt} = k \nabla^2 T + \Phi + Q_{source}$$ [cite: 62, 68]
$c_p$ = specific heat | $k$ = thermal conductivity | $\Phi$ = viscous dissipation [cite: 69]

SECTION 02 [cite: 96]

Modes of Heat Transfer [cite: 97]

How Energy Moves Through Matter and Space [cite: 98]

Conduction — Heat Through Solids [cite: 99]

Fourier's Law | No bulk motion required [cite: 100]

  • Transfer through direct molecular vibration — no bulk fluid movement [cite: 101]
  • Occurs primarily in solids; also in liquids & gases (to a lesser extent) [cite: 102]
  • Negative sign: heat always flows from HOT → COLD (2nd Law) [cite: 105]
  • Examples: metal rod in flame, heat spreading through a wall, CPU heat sink [cite: 106]
  • Thermal conductivities: Copper 385 | Steel 50 | Glass 1.0 | Air 0.026 W/m·K [cite: 107]
$$Q = -kA \frac{dT}{dx}$$ [cite: 103, 108]
Heat Flux: $q = \frac{Q}{A} = -k \frac{dT}{dx}$ [W/m²] [cite: 108]
Higher $k$ = better conductor | Lower $k$ = better insulator [cite: 109]

Fourier's Law — Deep Dive [cite: 110]

Mathematical Foundation of Conductive Heat Transfer [cite: 111]

  • Proposed by Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier in 1822 (Théorie Analytique de la Chaleur) [cite: 112]
  • In 3D: $\vec{q} = -k\nabla T$ (heat flux vector points opposite to temperature gradient) [cite: 114]
  • For a flat wall (thickness $L$): $Q = kA(T_1-T_2)/L \rightarrow$ thermal resistance $R = L/(kA)$ [cite: 115]
  • Composite walls (insulation): $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$ (series resistance) [cite: 116]
  • Applications: building insulation, electronic heat sinks, furnace wall design [cite: 117]
  • Thermal diffusivity: $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$ — how fast temperature changes spread [cite: 118]
$q = -k\nabla T$ [cite: 119] $R = L/(kA)$ [cite: 119] $\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)$ [cite: 119]

Convection — Heat Through Fluid Motion [cite: 121]

Newton's Law of Cooling | Forced & Natural Convection [cite: 122]

  • Heat transfer via BULK movement of fluid (liquid or gas) [cite: 123]
  • $h$ = convective heat transfer coefficient (W/m²·K) — depends on fluid, flow, geometry [cite: 125]
  • FORCED Convection: external mechanism (fan, pump) drives fluid — higher $h$ values [cite: 126]
  • NATURAL (Free) Convection: driven by buoyancy — density differences [cite: 127]
  • Examples: car radiator, air conditioning, blood circulation [cite: 129]
$$Q = hA(T_w - T_\infty)$$ [cite: 124, 130]
$T_w$ = wall temperature | $T_\infty$ = bulk fluid temperature [cite: 131]
$h$ varies: 5 (free air) → 100,000 W/m²K (boiling) [cite: 128, 130]

Natural Convection — Driven by Buoyancy [cite: 132]

Grashof Number · Rayleigh Number · Circulation Loops [cite: 133]

  • Heated fluid expands, density decreases → rises (buoyant); cooler fluid sinks → circulation loop [cite: 134]
  • No external pump/fan — driven purely by gravity + density difference [cite: 135]
  • Grashof Number: $Gr = g\beta(T_s-T_\infty)L^3 / \nu^2$ [cite: 136]
  • $\beta$ = thermal expansion coefficient | $\nu$ = kinematic viscosity | $L$ = characteristic length [cite: 137]
  • High $Gr \rightarrow$ convection dominates | Low $Gr \rightarrow$ conduction dominates [cite: 138]
  • Rayleigh Number: $Ra = Gr \times Pr$ (Prandtl number $Pr = \nu/\alpha$) [cite: 139]
  • Real examples: atmospheric circulation, sea breezes, room heating, lava lamps [cite: 140]
$Gr = \frac{g\beta(T_s-T_\infty)L^3}{\nu^2}$ [cite: 141] $Ra = Gr \times Pr$ [cite: 141]

Radiation — Heat Without a Medium [cite: 154]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law | Electromagnetic Heat Transfer [cite: 155]

  • Transfer via electromagnetic waves (photons) — requires NO medium (works in vacuum) [cite: 156]
  • Can travel at speed of light — e.g. solar energy crossing 150 million km to Earth [cite: 157]
  • $\epsilon$ = Emissivity: 0 (perfect reflector) → 1 (ideal blackbody) [cite: 160]
  • $T$ must be in KELVIN — a body at 2× the temperature radiates $2^4 = 16\times$ more energy [cite: 161]
  • Net radiation between surfaces: $Q = \epsilon\sigma A(T_1^4 - T_2^4)$ [cite: 162]
$$Q = \epsilon\sigma AT^4$$ [cite: 158, 163]
$\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W/m}^2\text{K}^4$ [cite: 159, 163]
Radiation dominant at high temperatures [cite: 164]

SECTION 04 [cite: 233]

Boundary Layer Flow [cite: 234]

Where Fluid Meets Surface — The Thin Viscous Region [cite: 235]

The Boundary Layer Concept [cite: 236]

Ludwig Prandtl 1904 · No-Slip Condition · Viscous Effects [cite: 237]

  • Introduced by Ludwig Prandtl at Heidelberg Congress, 1904 — revolutionized fluid mechanics [cite: 238]
  • Thin region adjacent to solid surface where viscous effects dominate [cite: 239]
  • NO-SLIP CONDITION: fluid particles at wall have zero velocity ($u_{wall} = 0$) [cite: 240]
  • Velocity transitions from 0 (at wall) to $U_\infty$ (free-stream) across the boundary layer [cite: 241]
  • Thickness $\delta$: defined as location where $u = 0.99 \times U_\infty$ [cite: 242]
  • Outside boundary layer: inviscid (potential flow) region — viscosity negligible [cite: 243]
  • Prandtl's insight: divide flow into two regions → enables analytical solutions [cite: 244]
No-slip: $u(y=0) = 0$   |   BL thickness: $\delta$ where $u(\delta) = 0.99 U_\infty$ [cite: 245]

Boundary Layer Separation [cite: 284]

Adverse Pressure Gradient · Stall · Wake · Prevention [cite: 285]

  • Occurs when BL detaches from surface due to ADVERSE PRESSURE GRADIENT ($dP/dx > 0$) [cite: 286]
  • Low-energy fluid near wall cannot overcome rising pressure → flow reverses direction [cite: 287]
  • CONSEQUENCES: increased pressure drag, loss of lift (airfoil stall), wake formation, vibration [cite: 289]
  • Triggered by: sharp corners, sudden expansions, bluff bodies, high angle of attack [cite: 290]
  • PREVENTION: streamlined body shapes, vortex generators, surface suction slots [cite: 291]
  • Turbulent BL separates LATER than laminar (better mixing re-energizes near-wall fluid) [cite: 292]
Separation point: wall shear stress $\tau_w = \mu \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)_{wall} = 0$ [cite: 288, 293]
Wing stall ($\alpha > \alpha_{crit}$) is boundary layer separation at high angles of attack [cite: 294]
